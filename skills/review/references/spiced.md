# SPICED

SPICED is a qualification and discovery framework popularized by Winning by Design. It maps to the customer's journey rather than the seller's checklist.

The five dimensions:

| Letter | Dimension | What you're trying to learn |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | **Situation** | Their current state - team, stack, scale, process |
| **P** | **Pain** | What is specifically broken or painful right now |
| **I** | **Impact** | The quantified business cost of that pain |
| **C** | **Critical Event** | The forcing function compelling a decision now |
| **D** | **Decision** | The decision process, criteria, and stakeholders |

## Dimension definitions, scoring, and discovery patterns

---

### S - Situation

**What it is:** The factual context of the prospect's world. Team size, current tools, current process, current results, who's doing what.

**Why it matters:** Without situation, every recommendation you make later is a guess. But situation is also where weak reps spend 80% of the call - it's safe and easy.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Walk me through how this works today, end to end."
- "Who owns this process? How long have they been doing it that way?"
- "What does success look like for your team this quarter?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Rep didn't establish basic context. Doesn't know team size, current tools, or current process. Makes assumptions.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Rep got surface-level facts but didn't probe how things actually work day-to-day. Missing the "why" behind the setup.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Rep can describe the prospect's current operation in detail. Knows who does what, what tools they use, what their current results look like.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep uncovered a structural detail the prospect themselves hadn't articulated - a hidden dependency, an unowned process, a metric they don't track but should.

**Common mistakes:**
- Spending too much time here because it's comfortable
- Asking situation questions the rep could have answered from LinkedIn or the website
- Failing to connect situation back to pain ("you told me X about your setup - is that working?")

---

### P - Pain

**What it is:** What is specifically broken, painful, or below where it should be. Pain must be **specific, owned, and admitted by the prospect** - not inferred by the rep.

**Why it matters:** No pain, no deal. If the prospect doesn't say it hurts, it doesn't.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What made you take this meeting?"
- "When did you first notice this was a problem?"
- "What have you tried to fix it? What didn't work?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how painful is this today?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No pain surfaced. Prospect described their situation but never said anything was broken. Or rep stated pain hypothetically ("most companies struggle with...") and prospect didn't confirm.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Vague pain admitted ("we want to grow faster", "things could be better"). Not specific enough to act on. No emotional weight.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific, named pain with examples. Prospect described a concrete situation where the pain showed up. Rep got them to repeat or expand on it.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Prospect named a pain they hadn't said out loud before. You can hear them processing it as they speak. Rep stayed silent and let them sit in it.

**Common mistakes:**
- Naming the pain for the prospect ("so it sounds like X is the issue") - they nod but don't own it
- Accepting "we want to grow" as pain - it's a goal, not pain
- Moving on after one pain quote instead of digging for the second-order pain underneath

---

### I - Impact

**What it is:** The quantified business consequence of the pain. Revenue lost, cost added, time wasted, opportunities missed - in numbers.

**Why it matters:** This is what justifies your price. A pain without impact is a complaint. A pain with quantified impact is a business case.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What's that costing you - in revenue, time, or headcount?"
- "If this isn't solved in 6 months, what happens to your numbers?"
- "How much time does your team spend on this every week?"
- "What's a closed-won deal worth to you on average?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Zero quantification. Pain was named but no numbers attached.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Soft numbers ("a lot of time", "thousands of dollars"). Not concrete enough to anchor pricing against.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific numbers extracted - hours per week, dollars per month, deal size, conversion rate, headcount cost. Tied to a business outcome.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep got the prospect to do the math out loud. "So that's $X per month? Times 12 months? So this is a $Y/year problem." Prospect agreed with the math.

**Common mistakes:**
- Calculating impact for the prospect instead of getting them to say the number
- Accepting the first vague number ("a lot") instead of pushing for specifics
- Focusing on cost-impact only and missing revenue/opportunity impact

---

### C - Critical Event

**What it is:** A specific date or event creating urgency. A board meeting, a renewal date, a contract expiry, a quarterly target, a launch, a hiring freeze, a product release.

**Why it matters:** Without a critical event, "Q2 sometime" becomes "Q3 sometime" becomes "next year." A critical event is what compresses the decision cycle.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What happens if this isn't solved by [their stated timeframe]?"
- "Is there a board meeting, renewal, or deadline tied to this?"
- "What's driving the timeline? Why now and not 6 months ago, or 6 months from now?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No timeline discussed, or prospect said "no rush" / "exploring" / "Q2 sometime" and rep didn't push.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Soft timeline ("hoping to have something in place by end of quarter") with no consequence attached.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific event with a date and a stated consequence if missed. "Our renewal with [vendor] is March 31. If we don't have a replacement by then, we auto-renew for another year."
- **10 (Exceptional):** Critical event tied to the prospect's personal stake (their bonus, their commitment to their boss, their first 90-day plan). You know what they personally lose if this slips.

**Common mistakes:**
- Accepting "as soon as possible" as a critical event - it's not
- Asking "what's your timeline?" instead of "what's driving the timeline?"
- Letting the prospect set a vague deadline without pushing for the consequence

---

### D - Decision

**What it is:** Three things together - the decision **process** (how they buy), the decision **criteria** (what they evaluate on), and the decision **makers** (who actually signs).

**Why it matters:** You can have all four other dimensions perfect and still lose the deal because you sold to the wrong person, missed a stakeholder, or didn't know there was a procurement step.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Walk me through how a decision like this gets made at your company."
- "Who else needs to be involved in this evaluation?"
- "What would make you choose us over [competitor]? What would make you not?"
- "Has your company bought something like this before? How did that go?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Rep doesn't know who the decision-maker is, what the criteria are, or how procurement works. Assumed the person on the call could decide.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Rep knows the title of the decision-maker but hasn't met them. Has a vague sense of criteria. Didn't ask about procurement or legal.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Rep knows: who decides, who influences, what they evaluate on, what the procurement process is, what the typical timeline from yes to signed contract looks like.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep got a commitment to a multi-thread next step (intro to additional stakeholder, kickoff with procurement, evaluation criteria document). The path to close is mapped.

**Common mistakes:**
- Asking "are you the decision-maker?" - they always say yes
- Assuming the loudest person on the call decides
- Skipping procurement/legal/security questions and getting blindsided after verbal commit

---

## How to score the call overall

Score each of the five dimensions independently. Don't average them in your head before scoring.

After all five are scored, identify the single dimension with the lowest score AND the highest leverage. That's the biggest gap. (Sometimes the lowest score isn't the biggest gap - e.g., a 2 on Decision matters more than a 2 on Situation if the deal is otherwise advanced.)

The next move should target the biggest gap directly with a specific question or action.
