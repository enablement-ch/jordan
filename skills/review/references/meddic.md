# MEDDIC

MEDDIC is a complex-deal qualification framework popularized by Jack Napoli at PTC. It is the dominant methodology in enterprise B2B SaaS sales. The expanded version is MEDDPICC (adds Paper Process and Competition).

The six dimensions:

| Letter | Dimension | What you're trying to learn |
|---|---|---|
| **M** | **Metrics** | The quantified business impact of solving the problem |
| **E** | **Economic Buyer** | The single person with discretionary spend authority |
| **D** | **Decision Criteria** | The explicit criteria the prospect will evaluate vendors on |
| **D** | **Decision Process** | The exact steps from now to signed contract |
| **I** | **Identify Pain** | The specific, owned, actively-felt business problem |
| **C** | **Champion** | The internal advocate selling for you when you're not in the room |

## Dimension definitions, scoring, and discovery patterns

---

### M - Metrics

**What it is:** The quantified economic impact of solving the problem - in revenue, cost, time, or risk. Numbers the prospect agrees with and can articulate themselves.

**Why it matters:** Metrics are how the buyer justifies the spend internally. Without metrics, your champion has nothing to walk into a budget review with.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What would solving this be worth to the business in dollars?"
- "If we got you to [specific outcome], what would that translate to in revenue or cost saved?"
- "How will you measure whether this investment paid off in 6 months?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No quantification. The buyer described the problem but didn't attach numbers.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Soft numbers ("a lot of time", "thousands of dollars"). Not tied to a specific business outcome.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific dollar/percentage/time numbers tied to revenue, cost, or risk. Buyer agreed with the math.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Buyer did the math out loud and committed to the number. "So that's $X per month, $Y per year - yes, that's what we're losing."

**Common mistakes:**
- Calculating metrics for the buyer instead of getting them to do the math
- Stopping at the first vague number ("a lot")
- Failing to convert output metrics (meetings, MQLs) into business metrics (revenue, ARR)

---

### E - Economic Buyer

**What it is:** The single individual with discretionary authority to release the budget for this purchase. Not a procurement rubber-stamper - the person whose unilateral yes makes the deal happen.

**Why it matters:** You can have a perfect deal with a champion who doesn't have authority and lose to a worse competitor whose rep got to the EB. The Economic Buyer is the deal.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Who has the authority to release the budget for this if you said yes today?"
- "Has [name] been involved in similar decisions? When was the last one and how did it go?"
- "When does [name] usually get involved - early in the process or only at signing?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Rep doesn't know who the EB is. Has assumed the person on the call has authority without confirming.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Rep knows the EB's name and title but has not met them. Doesn't know what the EB cares about.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Rep has met the EB or has a clear path to meet them. Knows what metrics the EB is measured on and what they care about in this decision.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep has had a direct conversation with the EB about this specific deal and the EB has signaled support or stated their criteria.

**Common mistakes:**
- Assuming title equals authority (a "VP" at one company is the EB; at another, they need 3 layers of approval)
- Accepting "I make these decisions" without testing it ("when was the last $X decision you made unilaterally?")
- Not asking until late in the process - by then, scheduling the EB conversation can take weeks

---

### D - Decision Criteria

**What it is:** The explicit criteria the buyer will use to evaluate vendors - features, capabilities, references, security, integrations, price, etc. The list they're scoring you against (whether they've written it down or not).

**Why it matters:** If you don't know the criteria, you can't position against them. Worse, the criteria are usually shaped early - whoever shaped them has a structural advantage.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What are the must-haves vs. nice-to-haves for the solution you pick?"
- "If you had to score vendors A, B, and C on a 1-10 scale, what dimensions would you score them on?"
- "What would make you NOT choose us? What would tip you toward [competitor]?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Rep doesn't know the criteria. Pitching features hoping something lands.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Rep knows 2-3 obvious criteria (price, features) but not the full list, weighting, or who set it.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Rep has the full list of criteria, knows which are must-have vs. nice-to-have, knows which competitor is strong on which.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep helped shape the criteria - the buyer's evaluation rubric has the rep's preferred dimensions on it.

**Common mistakes:**
- Asking "what are you looking for?" and accepting the marketing-website answer
- Not testing the criteria for hidden weights (the official criteria says "price 10%" but procurement actually rules out anyone above $X)
- Failing to ask what would DISqualify you

---

### D - Decision Process

**What it is:** The exact, step-by-step path from "we like this" to "signed contract." Who has to approve, what documents are required, how long each step takes, what triggers each step.

**Why it matters:** Most "stuck" deals aren't stuck on the merits - they're stuck in an unmapped procurement, legal, or security review the rep didn't surface. Mapping the process is how you compress the cycle.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Walk me through the steps from a verbal yes today to a signed contract."
- "What's typically involved in procurement at your size? Security review? Legal redlines?"
- "How long did your last vendor purchase of similar size take from yes to signed?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Rep has no idea how a deal gets done. "We just need to get on a call with the boss."
- **4-6 (Passable):** Rep knows the high-level path (verbal yes → contract → sign) but doesn't know the steps in between.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Rep has a documented mutual action plan with dates, owners, and steps. Both sides agree to it.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep has shortened the typical process by getting procurement, legal, and security started in parallel rather than serial.

**Common mistakes:**
- Assuming "we'll get a contract over to you" is the whole process
- Surprised by procurement, legal, or InfoSec at the end
- Letting the buyer drive the timeline without surfacing all the steps

---

### I - Identify Pain

**What it is:** The specific, owned, actively-felt business problem the prospect is trying to solve. Not a generic want ("we want to grow"). A real pain that has a cost and an owner.

**Why it matters:** No pain, no deal. MEDDIC's "I" is identical in spirit to SPICED's Pain - the rule is the same: the buyer must say it hurts, in their own words, with specifics.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What made you take this meeting?"
- "What's the cost of leaving this unsolved for another 6 months?"
- "Walk me through the last time this problem cost you something concrete."

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No pain surfaced. Buyer described the situation but never said anything was broken.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Vague pain ("things could be better"). No specificity, no emotional weight.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific, named pain with examples. Buyer described concrete moments where it hurt. Buyer owns it.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Buyer named a pain they hadn't articulated before. You can hear them processing it as they speak.

**Common mistakes:**
- Naming pain for the buyer ("so it sounds like X is the issue") - they nod but don't own it
- Accepting "we want to grow" or "we want to be more efficient" as pain
- Moving on after one pain quote instead of digging for the second-order pain underneath

---

### C - Champion

**What it is:** A specific individual inside the buyer's organization who (a) has power or influence, (b) personally benefits if your solution gets bought, and (c) is willing to sell for you when you're not in the room.

**Why it matters:** Complex deals are won or lost in conversations the rep is never in. The champion is the person carrying the deal forward in those conversations.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What does this look like for [name] specifically if it works? What does it look like if we don't do it?"
- "Who else in the company is excited about solving this?"
- "If we proposed today, who would be the loudest voice for it internally?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No champion identified. Rep is the only person carrying the deal.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Someone seems supportive but rep hasn't tested whether they'll fight for the deal internally.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific person identified, has power/influence, has a personal win, and has been observed advocating for the deal.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Champion is actively coaching the rep on how to win - sharing internal politics, helping shape the criteria, naming objections before they surface.

**Common mistakes:**
- Mistaking a coach (someone who likes you) for a champion (someone who fights for you)
- Not testing the champion - asking them to do something hard early ("can you set up a meeting with [EB] this week?") to see if they'll act
- Having only one champion - if they leave or get reorged, the deal dies

---

## How to score the call overall

Score each of the six dimensions independently. Don't average them in your head before scoring.

After all six are scored, identify the single dimension with the lowest score AND the highest leverage. That's the biggest gap. Champion and Economic Buyer are typically the highest-leverage dimensions in complex deals - a 4 there matters more than a 4 in Decision Criteria.

The next move should target the biggest gap directly with a specific question or action.
