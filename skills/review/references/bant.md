# BANT

BANT is the original qualification framework, developed at IBM in the 1960s. It is the simplest of the three and remains the most-used framework in transactional and SMB sales.

The four dimensions:

| Letter | Dimension | What you're trying to learn |
|---|---|---|
| **B** | **Budget** | Do they have money allocated, and at what tier |
| **A** | **Authority** | Are we talking to the person who can decide |
| **N** | **Need** | Do they have a real, specific need |
| **T** | **Timing** | Is there a forcing function compelling a decision now |

**A note on BANT vs. modern frameworks:** BANT is criticized as too vendor-centric (qualifying the prospect FOR YOU rather than understanding their world). It also misses concepts like Champion and Decision Criteria that complex deals require. Use BANT for transactional deals, short cycles, or as a sanity-check overlay on more sophisticated frameworks. For complex enterprise deals, MEDDIC or SPICED will give a better picture.

## Dimension definitions, scoring, and discovery patterns

---

### B - Budget

**What it is:** Whether the buyer has financial capacity for the solution at the price you're going to quote, and whether that capacity is allocated specifically (line item, project budget) or theoretical (could maybe find it).

**Why it matters:** A perfect-fit deal at the wrong tier is dead. Discovering budget mismatch in the last call wastes everyone's time.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Do you have a budget allocated for this, or would it need to come from somewhere?"
- "What's the realistic range you'd consider for solving this?"
- "What did you spend on [related thing] last year?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No budget conversation at all. Rep is hoping for a number to magically appear at proposal time.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Soft budget signal ("we have some budget", "we'd find it for the right solution"). Not specific enough to size against.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific budget range or allocation confirmed. Rep knows whether their pricing is in the green, yellow, or red zone.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Buyer has confirmed a specific allocated budget at or above the rep's proposed price. The conversation is now about value, not affordability.

**Common mistakes:**
- Avoiding the budget question to avoid awkwardness - then getting blindsided by a pricing rejection
- Accepting "we have budget" without sizing it
- Confusing "we could find money" with "we have money allocated"

---

### A - Authority

**What it is:** Whether the person on the call has the authority to say yes, or whether someone else (or several someone-elses) ultimately makes the call. In modern sales, "authority" is closer to MEDDIC's Economic Buyer concept.

**Why it matters:** Selling brilliantly to someone who can't decide is unpaid education. Every deal needs to either be in front of the decision-maker or have a clear path to them.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Who else needs to be involved in this decision?"
- "If we got to a yes today, who else has to weigh in before contracts?"
- "Has [name] been involved in similar decisions before?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** Rep doesn't know who decides. Assumed the person on the call has authority without confirming. Or rep asked "are you the decision-maker?" and accepted "yes."
- **4-6 (Passable):** Rep knows the decision-maker's title but hasn't met them. Vague sense of who else is involved.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Rep knows everyone who has to weigh in, has met or has clear path to the decision-maker, and understands who has veto power.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Rep is in direct conversation with the decision-maker and has confirmed support or surfaced their criteria.

**Common mistakes:**
- Asking "are you the decision-maker?" - they always say yes
- Treating an enthusiastic mid-level contact as an authority because they're easy to talk to
- Not surfacing veto-holders (procurement, legal, IT) until proposal stage

---

### N - Need

**What it is:** Whether there is a real, specific business need - not a generic want. Closer to SPICED's Pain or MEDDIC's Identify Pain. The buyer must articulate the problem in their own words with specifics.

**Why it matters:** Deals without need are deals where the buyer is "just exploring." Those deals close at near-zero rates.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "What made you take this meeting?"
- "What's the cost of NOT solving this?"
- "Walk me through how this problem shows up in your week."

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No need surfaced. Buyer is "just exploring" or "evaluating options" without a specific problem.
- **4-6 (Passable):** Vague need ("we want to grow", "we'd like to be more efficient"). No emotional weight, no specifics.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific, named need with examples. Buyer described concrete moments where the problem hurt.
- **10 (Exceptional):** Buyer surfaced a need they hadn't articulated before. You can hear them processing it.

**Common mistakes:**
- Accepting "we want X" as need - that's a feature request, not a need
- Naming the need for the buyer
- Stopping at the first pain quote without going deeper

---

### T - Timing

**What it is:** Whether there is a specific forcing function compelling a decision now - a date, an event, a contract expiry, a milestone. Closest to SPICED's Critical Event.

**Why it matters:** Without timing, "interested" stays "interested" forever. Timing is what compresses cycles and prevents deals from sliding indefinitely.

**Good discovery looks like:**
- "Why now and not 6 months ago, or 6 months from now?"
- "What's driving the timeline? Is there an event or deadline?"
- "What happens if this isn't solved by [their stated date]?"

**Scoring anchors:**
- **1-3 (Poor):** No timeline. "Whenever we find the right fit." "Q2 sometime." "ASAP."
- **4-6 (Passable):** Soft timeline ("hoping to have something by end of quarter") with no consequence attached.
- **7-9 (Strong):** Specific event, specific date, specific consequence if missed. "Our renewal is March 31; if we don't have a replacement we auto-renew for a year."
- **10 (Exceptional):** Timing tied to the buyer's personal stake (their bonus, board commitment, first-90-days plan). You know what THEY personally lose if it slips.

**Common mistakes:**
- Accepting "as soon as possible" as a timeline (it's not)
- Asking "what's your timeline?" instead of "what's driving the timeline?"
- Letting the buyer set a vague date without surfacing the consequence

## How to score the call overall

Score each of the four dimensions independently.

BANT's biggest weakness is that all four dimensions are weighted equally in the framework, but in reality they're not. In modern sales:

- **Need** and **Timing** are usually the highest-leverage dimensions - without them, the deal isn't real regardless of Budget and Authority.
- **Authority** matters most in deals where the wrong person on the call can waste weeks.
- **Budget** matters most when your pricing has multiple tiers and you're choosing which to quote.

When identifying the biggest gap, weight by impact, not by score alone.
